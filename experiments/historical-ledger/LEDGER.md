# Historical projection-gap ledger

Natural experiments where a **non-monetary coordinate moved while monetary incentives were held
fixed (or moved the *other* way)**, and where **standard (scalar/monetary) economics is forced to
predict the wrong sign**. Each row is a falsifiable retrodiction: the geometry earns a point only if
it predicts the observed *direction* that scalar theory gets backwards.

This is retrodiction done honestly — not narrative "the geometry explains history," but sign-level
predictions on documented episodes where the two theories **provably diverge**. It is the same logic
as the projection-gap experiment, read off real-world data we did not collect.

> **Scope discipline.** Sign-level only (magnitude is not yet calibrated — see the temperature issue).
> No grand macro / crisis "we called it" claims — those are unfalsifiable and excluded by design.
> Coordinates: d1 Monetary, d6 Social Impact, d7 Virtue/Identity, d8 Legitimacy, d9 Epistemic Status.

## Class A — Motivation crowding-out (d1 ↔ d6/d7)

The core case, and *exactly* the TCSS money-dimension corollary: raising the monetary incentive (d1)
displaces the social-impact / identity frame (d6/d7), so behaviour **reverses**. Scalar economics
predicts monotone improvement; the geometry predicts the sign flip.

| Episode | Money moved | Coordinate displaced | Scalar predicts | Geometry predicts | Observed | Geometry wins? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Blood donation for pay** (Titmuss 1970) | + (introduce payment) | d6/d7 (gift → commodity) | supply ↑ | supply ↓ | supply **↓** | ✓ |
| **"A Fine is a Price"** — daycare late-pickup fine (Gneezy & Rustichini 2000a, *J. Legal Studies*) | + (add fine for lateness) | d7/d8 (moral obligation → market price) | lateness ↓ | lateness ↑ | lateness **↑** (and stayed up after removal) | ✓ |
| **"Pay Enough or Don't Pay at All"** (Gneezy & Rustichini 2000b, *QJE*) | + (small payment for effort) | d7 (volunteer identity) | effort ↑ | effort ↓ vs unpaid | effort **↓** at low pay | ✓ |
| **Nuclear-waste siting compensation** (Frey & Oberholzer-Gee 1997, *AER*) | + (offer money to accept siting) | d8/d6 (civic duty → transaction) | acceptance ↑ | acceptance ↓ | acceptance **↓** (51% → 25%) | ✓ |
| **Cash for grades / test scores** (Fryer 2011 and related) | + (pay for outcomes) | d7 (learning identity) | outcomes ↑ | ~null / ↓ on outcomes | outputs paid ↑, **outcomes ~null** | partial (sign for outcomes) |

**Reading:** in every case scalar economics predicts the wrong direction on the target behaviour, and
the geometry's d1→d6/d7 displacement predicts the observed reversal. This is a whole *class* of
existing sign-level wins, and it is the strongest historical validation available because the
competitor is not merely imprecise — it is **backwards**.

## How to read this ledger

- Each ✓ is a case where **scalar economics predicts the wrong sign and the geometry predicts the
  right one.** That is a stronger claim than "better fit."
- These are **retrodictions**, so the encoding of the moved coordinate should ideally be pre-committed;
  where an episode is famous and the coordinate is unambiguous (Titmuss = gift/commodity; Wall
  St/Community = social frame), the retrodiction is clean.
- The crowding-out class (A) is the flagship: it is a documented, replicated, sign-level failure of
  scalar economics that the geometry's d1↔d6/d7 displacement predicts, and it *is* the TCSS
  money-dimension corollary observed in the wild.
- **What this ledger is not:** proof the geometry is the unique correct model. Other frameworks
  (intrinsic-motivation theory, social-preference models) also predict some of these. The geometry's
  distinct claim is that **one shared metric** predicts all four classes; that unification, not any
  single row, is the contribution.
