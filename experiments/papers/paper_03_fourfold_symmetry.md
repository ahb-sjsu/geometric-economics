# The Fourfold Pattern is a Group: Klein-Four Symmetry of Risk Attitudes with a Loss-Aversion Field

*Structural paper. Draft. States a group-theoretic organizing principle for the Tversky–Kahneman
fourfold pattern, derived from choice data, and is candid about the line between genuine structure and
re-description. The load-bearing content is a single empirical constraint (probability has no main
effect on risk attitude) and a cross-method convergence (two independent analyses recover the same
loss-aversion term). Everything else is either that content or honest boundary-marking.*

---

## Abstract

The fourfold pattern of risk attitudes — risk aversion for likely gains and unlikely losses, risk
seeking for unlikely gains and likely losses — is among the most replicated regularities in
behavioral economics. We show it is not four independent facts but the action of the **Klein
four-group V₄ = (ℤ/2)²** generated by two commuting involutions: a **value reflection** σ_v (gain ↔
loss) and a **probability reflection** σ_p (certainty ↔ possibility). Empirically, the risk attitude
is the **product d·q** of the domain and probability signs — a pure interaction — with the
probability *main* effect statistically indistinguishable from zero (the non-trivial constraint; a
pure involution has no offset). The symmetry is broken by a single term, **loss aversion**, which
appears as the domain main effect. Two independent analyses — a polar reflection encoding of the
gain/loss mirror, and the interaction decomposition of the fourfold cells — recover the **same**
loss-aversion magnitude (a −0.8 mirror instead of a perfect −1; a domain main effect of the matching
size). We further test whether V₄ lifts to the full dihedral group D₄ (a 90° rotation mixing the two
axes) and find it does **not**: with mixed gambles filling the interior of the (domain, probability)
plane, the rotation-covariant term vanishes and rotation-breaking terms dominate. The structure is a
**rectangle (V₄), not a square (D₄)** — the value and probability axes are genuinely different kinds
of thing. We are explicit about what is structure and what is re-description, and about the two-corpus
evidentiary base.

---

## 1. The question

The fourfold pattern (Tversky & Kahneman 1992) is a 2×2: risk attitude as a function of **domain**
(gains vs losses) and **probability** (high/certain vs low/possible). Cumulative prospect theory
accounts for it with two machines — a value function with diminishing sensitivity and reference
dependence, and an inverse-S probability weighting function. That account is predictive but
*compositional*: two mechanisms whose product happens to reproduce the four cells.

We ask a structural question instead: **does the 2×2 carry a group?** A 2×2 with two binary flips
always admits a formal Klein-four labeling; the scientific content is not the labeling but whether the
*data* obey the group's constraints — in particular whether the risk attitude is a **pure interaction**
(the group's defining representation) rather than a sum of independent effects, and whether the two
flips are genuine involutions of the choice geometry.

## 2. Theory: V₄, its irreducible representations, and the D₄ question

Place the four cells at the corners of a square, coordinates (d, q) ∈ {±1}² with d = +1 gain / −1 loss
and q = +1 certainty / −1 possibility. Two involutions:

- **σ_v** (value reflection): d → −d. Swaps gains and losses.
- **σ_p** (probability reflection): q → −q. Swaps certainty and possibility.

If they commute, ⟨σ_v, σ_p⟩ = V₄. The 4-dimensional space of functions on the corners decomposes into
V₄ (equivalently D₄) irreducibles:

- **A₁** (trivial): the mean.
- **B** (the *pseudoscalar* d·q): the sign that flips under either reflection and is fixed under both —
  the **chirality**.
- **E** (the standard 2-D rep, spanned by d and q): the components that distinguish the two axes.

The fourfold pattern's signature is that the **risk attitude lives in the B (pseudoscalar) irrep**:
risk attitude ∝ d·q. That is the precise, falsifiable form of "it is a group and not four facts" —
the two *main* effects (the E rep) must vanish; only the interaction (B) survives.

**Lift to D₄.** V₄ is the reflection subgroup of the dihedral group D₄; the extra generator is the 90°
rotation r (order 4), r² = σ_v σ_p, which *mixes* the two axes. D₄ (a square) requires the two axes to
be equivalent — the E rep, and the quadratic d²−q², must both vanish. A rectangle has only V₄.

## 3. Methods

- **Interaction decomposition (corners).** Pool the 17 Kahneman–Tversky problems replicated by Ruggeri
  et al. (2020) across 19 countries into one conditional logit in which the risk coefficient on ΔSD is
  written `b₀ + b_d·d + b_q·q + b_dq·(d·q)`. `b_dq` is the B (pseudoscalar) amplitude; `(b_d, b_q)` is
  the E amplitude. Bootstrap over countries.
- **Reflection encoding (independent).** Fit a single geometric metric across gains and losses with a
  per-coordinate reflection coefficient (the loss domain as the gain domain mirrored across the value
  axis). The value-axis reflection strength ρ_SD is an *independent* estimate of the same loss-aversion
  asymmetry.
- **Continuous rotation (interior).** Add CPC18's mixed (gain-and-loss) gambles to fill the interior of
  the (d, q) plane and fit the risk coefficient with D₄-allowed terms {1, d²+q², d·q} versus
  rotation-breaking terms {d, q, d²−q²}. A genuine rotation must govern the interior, not just the
  corners.

## 4. Results

**The interaction dominates (V₄).** `b_dq = +0.80` (95% CI [0.68, 0.91]) — 3.4× the summed main
effects. The risk attitude is, to leading order, the pseudoscalar d·q.

**Probability has no main effect (the non-trivial constraint).** `b_q = +0.004` (CI [−0.05, 0.05]):
zero. Probability level acts *only* through its interaction with domain — exactly how a pure involution
generator behaves, and a constraint that a compositional (separate weighting × value) account does not
require.

**σ_v is a clean involution; loss aversion is the sole breaking term.** The value reflection flips the
risk attitude cleanly (8% residual). The one non-vanishing E component is the **domain** main effect
`b_d = +0.20` — loss aversion. The two axes are therefore not symmetric.

**Cross-method convergence.** The reflection encoding independently returns **ρ_SD = −0.80** — a
*partial* mirror, short of a perfect −1 by an amount that is loss aversion. A polar reflection fit and
an interaction decomposition, sharing no machinery, recover the **same** symmetry-breaking term. This
convergence, not either number alone, is the paper's strongest evidence that the structure is real.

**No lift to D₄.** At the corners the chirality is 80% of the structure, but the E component is
significant (likelihood-ratio χ² = 75, p = 4×10⁻¹⁷; the V₄/free model beats the D₄-constrained model on
BIC). In the interior, the rotation-covariant d·q term **vanishes** (+0.003) while rotation-breaking
terms dominate (d²−q² = +0.34). The fourfold d·q is a **corner** phenomenon; there is no continuous 90°
symmetry. **Rectangle, not square.**

## 5. Discussion — structure or re-description?

We hold ourselves to the standard of the keystone paper: a formal relabeling is scientifically empty;
only a constraint the data *could have violated* is content.

- **Empty (stated to disarm it):** that a 2×2 admits a Klein-four labeling is automatic.
- **Content:** that the risk attitude is a *pure interaction* — `b_q ≈ 0`, the probability main effect
  vanishing — is a constraint the data could have failed and did not. Compositional prospect theory
  does not force it; a genuine two-involution group does.
- **Content:** the cross-method convergence on loss aversion (ρ_SD = −0.8 ≡ b_d) is a coincidence only
  if the structure is spurious.
- **Content (a negative):** the D₄ rejection is a real constraint on any theory (including the author's
  own symmetry-based framework) that would posit a rotation between the value and probability axes.

**Relation to prospect theory.** This is not a rival predictive model; CPT predicts choice
probabilities, and we do not beat it. It is an *organizing* claim: the fourfold pattern is one
group-with-a-field (V₄ + loss aversion), which is a more economical description than two independent
nonlinear functions, and which makes the additive cell constraints (probability sums equal; domain
sums differ by 2·loss-aversion) that a held-out corpus can test.

## 6. Limitations (plainly)

- **Two corpora.** Ruggeri (human, 19 countries) for the corners; CPC18/choices13k for reflection and
  the interior. Replication on a corpus *designed* to sample intermediate (domain × probability) angles
  is the clean way to settle the interior/rotation test.
- **Adversarial selection.** The KT problems are chosen to violate expected value (β_EV < 0), which
  makes the *absolute* risk-averse/seeking label per cell unreliable. The *structural* results
  (interaction dominance, b_q ≈ 0, the E residual) are sign-convention-independent; the per-cell
  attitude labels are not, and we do not lean on them.
- **Fitted, not yet pre-registered.** The V₄ decomposition and the reflection ρ are fits. The
  cross-method convergence is strong, but a pre-registered held-out re-test of the additive cell
  constraints would move this from "organizing description" to "confirmed structure."

## 7. Claim, exactly

The Tversky–Kahneman fourfold pattern is the **Klein four-group V₄** — two commuting reflections of the
choice geometry (value and probability) — perturbed by a **single loss-aversion field** that the same
data recover two independent ways. It does **not** lift to the dihedral D₄: there is no rotation
between the value and probability axes. We claim exactly that, and no more.
